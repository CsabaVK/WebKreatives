# OpenClaw Article Creation Guidelines

Created: 2026-05-18

Purpose: canonical guideline for OpenClaw-created WebKreatives articles.

Use this file whenever OpenClaw researches, drafts, edits, publishes, or repurposes WebKreatives articles.

## Main Goal

Every WebKreatives article should bring in customers by helping them first.

A good article should:
- answer a real question a potential client has;
- explain a website, SEO, trust, conversion, branding, or online-presence problem clearly;
- give practical next steps;
- support WebKreatives' positioning as a custom web design and development studio;
- create a strong LinkedIn post angle after publishing.

The reader should leave thinking:

"I understand the problem better now, and WebKreatives could help me fix it."

## Brand Position

WebKreatives builds custom websites for brands that have outgrown templates.

Article themes should reinforce:
- custom design over generic templates;
- practical website strategy;
- SEO foundations;
- trust and credibility;
- conversion-focused structure;
- website maintenance and support;
- AI-assisted modern workflows, without sounding like an AI hype brand.

## Required Research Rule

For every new WebKreatives article:

- Perform at least one focused web search before drafting.
- Prefer current sources from the last 12-24 months when the topic changes over time.
- Use primary or credible sources for statistics.
- Do not invent numbers, laws, trends, pricing, platform features, or market claims.
- Record the research sources in the article notes, draft file, or memory log.

This rule already matches the long-term WebKreatives memory preference: future articles must spend at least one web search/research credit before drafting so content is grounded in current external information.

## Article Strategy

Articles should not exist only for SEO.

Each article should have three jobs:

1. Help the reader.
2. Rank or be shareable for a specific search/social topic.
3. Create a LinkedIn post that can lead readers to the full article.

Before writing, define:

- Target reader.
- Problem they feel.
- Search intent.
- WebKreatives service connection.
- One practical takeaway.
- LinkedIn post angle.

## Good Article Topics

Prioritize topics that connect directly to customer pain.

Strong topics:
- why a website looks generic;
- template vs custom website;
- website redesign checklist;
- why visitors leave a website;
- homepage clarity;
- local SEO basics;
- Google Business Profile and website alignment;
- what to prepare before asking for a website quote;
- website cost and scope;
- landing page strategy;
- how reviews build trust;
- why AI website builders are useful but not always enough;
- what every service page should answer;
- how to make a website feel trustworthy;
- website maintenance after launch.

Weak topics:
- generic "why websites matter";
- vague digital transformation posts;
- trend lists with no practical advice;
- AI hype without buyer usefulness;
- articles that only promote WebKreatives.

## Article Angle Types

Choose one main angle.

### Diagnostic

Explains what is going wrong.

Example:
"5 reasons customers leave your website."

### Educational

Explains how something works.

Example:
"Local SEO: how Google finds you in your city."

### Buyer Guide

Helps the reader make a decision.

Example:
"What does a website cost in 2026?"

### Checklist

Gives immediate practical steps.

Example:
"Website redesign checklist before hiring a designer."

### Comparison

Clarifies tradeoffs.

Example:
"Template website vs custom website: what is the real difference?"

### Opinion / Studio POV

Shares WebKreatives' point of view.

Example:
"Why template-first websites make brands look interchangeable."

## Article Structure

Default structure:

1. SEO title.
2. Meta description.
3. Short introduction naming the reader's problem.
4. Clear explanation of why the topic matters.
5. Main sections with practical advice.
6. Examples or warning signs.
7. Checklist or summary.
8. Soft WebKreatives service bridge.
9. CTA.
10. Source notes.

## Title Rules

Good titles are clear, searchable, and specific.

Use:
- "Why..."
- "How..."
- "What..."
- "Checklist..."
- "Template vs Custom..."
- "X Reasons..."
- "X Signs..."

Avoid:
- clever but unclear titles;
- clickbait;
- titles that promise guaranteed results;
- titles too broad to rank or share.

Examples:
- "Template vs Custom Website: What Is the Real Difference?"
- "How to Know If Your Website Looks Trustworthy"
- "Website Redesign Checklist Before Hiring a Designer"
- "Why Your Homepage Is Not Converting Visitors"

## Slug Rules

Use English slugs:
- lowercase;
- hyphenated;
- no dates unless the date matters;
- no `.html` in public URLs.

Examples:
- `/articles/template-vs-custom-website/`
- `/articles/website-redesign-checklist/`
- `/articles/how-to-know-if-your-website-looks-trustworthy/`

## Language Rules

Current site behavior:
- Dutch is often visible in article body.
- English titles/meta are preferred for browser tabs and sharing.

Important long-term rule:
- When an article exists in multiple site languages, visible article content should match the selected language fully, not only cards/meta.
- If creating bilingual content, keep Dutch and English versions aligned.
- Do not leave mixed-language sections unless intentionally part of the site architecture.

## Research And Statistics

Use statistics only when they help the reader understand the issue.

Rules:
- Use current and credible sources.
- Prefer original sources over quote chains.
- Mention the source in plain language.
- Do not overload articles with stats.
- If using a statistic in LinkedIn later, make sure the article also contains or supports that source.

Good source types:
- Google documentation;
- LinkedIn resources;
- government/business data;
- reputable marketing studies;
- platform documentation;
- established industry publications.

Avoid:
- random blogs with no source;
- outdated stats;
- impossible-to-verify claims;
- "studies show" without naming the study.

## WebKreatives Service Bridge

Every article should include a soft bridge to WebKreatives.

Good:

"If your website feels generic or outdated, WebKreatives can help you plan a custom redesign around your brand, offer, and customer journey."

Bad:

"Contact us now for the best website in the world."

The bridge should feel like a natural next step, not an interruption.

## CTA Rules

Good CTAs:
- ask for a website review;
- invite a redesign conversation;
- point to the contact form;
- suggest reading a related article;
- invite the reader to compare their site against the checklist.

Avoid:
- pressure;
- fake urgency;
- guaranteed sales promises;
- huge claims against all agencies.

## SEO Requirements

Each article should include:
- unique title tag;
- unique meta description;
- canonical URL;
- Open Graph title;
- Open Graph description;
- Open Graph image if available;
- article schema where appropriate;
- datePublished and dateModified when used;
- clean internal links;
- related article links when relevant.

The public URL must be folder-based:

Good:

`/articles/local-seo-how-google-finds-you-in-your-city/`

Bad:

`/articles/local-seo-how-google-finds-you-in-your-city/index.html`

## LinkedIn Repurposing Requirement

Before an article is considered complete, create at least one LinkedIn post angle.

For each article, define:
- hook;
- practical value;
- exact article URL;
- one soft CTA;
- 3-5 hashtag options.

The post should follow:

`projects/04-WebKreatives/memory/OPENCLAW-LINKEDIN-AUTOMATION.md`

## Article Quality Gate

Before publishing, answer yes to all:

- Does the article solve a real customer question?
- Is the title clear and searchable?
- Is the advice practical?
- Are statistics sourced?
- Is the WebKreatives service connection natural?
- Is there a clear CTA?
- Is the article linkable from LinkedIn?
- Does the article avoid generic filler?
- Does it avoid overpromising?
- Are title/meta/canonical/OG tags updated?
- If bilingual, does the visible content match the selected language?

If any answer is no, revise before publishing.

## Suggested Article Workflow

1. Pick a topic from a real customer pain.
2. Define reader, problem, search intent, and WebKreatives service connection.
3. Run focused web research.
4. Save sources.
5. Draft outline.
6. Draft article.
7. Add practical examples/checklist.
8. Add soft WebKreatives bridge and CTA.
9. Add SEO metadata and schema.
10. Create LinkedIn post angle.
11. Verify article route and links.
12. Publish only when requested or when the task explicitly includes publishing.
13. Log the article in memory/daily notes.
